Designing for the Garmin User

The Connect IQ system allows third party developers to bring their design and experience to Garmin products. This document will go over some of the guidelines of designing an app for a Garmin audience.

Use Case Focused Devices

Unlike other platforms, Garmin products are targeted at the markets they serve:

  • The Edge series are the best cycling computers on the market.
  • The fēnix® series is the best wearable for the outdoors.
  • The Forerunner® series is the best wearable for runners and tri-athletes.
  • The vívoactive® and Venu® series brings the best of Garmin to an active-lifestyle audience.
  • The Oregon®, GPSMAP®, and Rino® series are the best handheld mapping products on the market.

Think about these distinctions when choosing which products to support.

The Wearable User

One of the synonyms of the word “watch” is “to observe”. When designing smart watch apps, it is best to keep in mind that watches are best when used to observe. Great wearable apps stay out of the user’s way, and keep the interactions to a minimum. Give the user fast access to the information and key interactions they care about.

Remember that wearables are the most personal computing platform ever created. Garmin devices have access to a number of different sensors, including GPS, compass, accelerometer, barometer, as well as the ANT+ network. Let the user see information about themselves in interesting ways.

The Cycling User

The Edge user needs their Edge to function flawlessly while they ride, regardless of duration of weather conditions. Create apps that integrate with the existing Garmin cycling experience rather than replacing the experience with your own. Users won’t like it if your apps lower their battery life no matter how great your experience is.

The Outdoor User

The outdoor user wants to be in the outdoors and not focused on your app experience. Focus on an experience that complements what they want to do. Design an autonomous experience that does not assume you are in a connected environment.

Common Use Cases

There are many reasons to make a Connect IQ app:

Integrating with Garmin Activities

Garmin is a trusted brand with athletes, with an audience that extends from aspirational to endurance. Connect IQ allows you to bring your brand or solution in front of this large audience.

Third-Party Sensors

Connect IQ allows third-party sensor makers to connect with to Garmin devices. Sensors can then record extra activity information that can be displayed in Garmin Connect.

Integrating Workout Content

Connect IQ allows developers to work their content into the Garmin activity experience.

New Activities

Garmin supports a wide range of activities, but with Connect IQ developers can extend the devices into workouts not supported natively like dance, in-line skating, or others.

Music Services

Connect IQ allows content providers to extend their services to Garmin users. Content can be downloaded over Wi-Fi and securely stored on our devices for later playback by users, and you can enforce your playback tracking and subscription management within the app.

Extending your Services to Garmin Customers

Garmin wearables are meant to be worn 24/7, and Connect IQ apps allow our users to access your services at a flick of the wrist.

Garmin as Part of a Broader Solution

Sometimes it’s necessary to architect a solution that involves a variety of technologies. With their programmability, sensors, and connectivity options, Connect IQ compatible devices can often serve as a part of a larger solution.

Customized Watch Faces

The watch face is not only the home screen of the Garmin wearable experience but also an opportunity for the user to express themselves. Custom watch faces allow you to bring your brand and creativity to our customers.

App Types

The Connect IQ app types are based on the use case of the app.

  • Watch faces provide custom displays for the home screen of the watch.
  • Device apps have full control of the device.
  • Widgets provide glanceable information to the user.
  • Data fields allow customization of the Garmin activities.
  • Audio content providers allow users to get your content in their ears.

Watch Faces

Watch faces act as the home screen for the wearable. They don’t allow input and have limits on when they can update to minimize power usage. They should show the time and date, but can also display information collected from sensors or cloud services.

Data Fields

Data fields allow developer to integrate their information with the Garmin activity experience. Developers have access to metrics collected on device as well as wirelessly connected external sensors, and information can be recorded into activity files for display on Garmin Connect. Data fields do not accept input.

When designing a data field keep in mind that the user can place them in their activity profiles. They can be full screen or one field among many on the screen. Design your field to scale based on how much visual area it has available.

Widgets

Widgets are glanceable apps that are launchable at any point. They allow the user quick access to information whatever they are doing. After a period of inaction widgets will time out to the home screen of the device. Widgets have limited input on the home screen but can push views that can take input.

Device Apps

Device apps are experiences launched by the user. Unlike widgets, device apps will run until the user explicitly exits them. Apps have the most access to the system, and do not have input limitations.

Best Practices

Some things to keep in mind when designing your app.

Put the Most Important Information Front and Center

Personal computer applications and mobile apps are often meant to be focused on for long periods of time, but Garmin devices are meant to be used to help the user beat yesterday. Your application is not meant to be the focus of their attention, but something to help them achieve their goals. Make sure you clearly place the relevant information they need on screen so they can return to what they need to do.

Keep Input to a Minimum

Garmin devices offer a wealth of information to the user to help with their performance and situational awareness, and all of this information should be available within three to four interactions with the device. The more they are interacting with your app the less they are paying attention to the task at hand.

Navigation

How the user navigates to a Connect IQ app depends on the type of app you create.

Device Apps

The two main interface patterns for Connect IQ device apps are hierarchical navigation and page loops.

Hierarchical Navigation

The traditional Garmin user interface is hierarchical, where views are stacked based on the user’s selections. Every device has a standard behavior for navigating the selections and backing out of the hierarchy, but these standard behaviors can be different from product to product.

Try to limit the depth of your navigational hierarchy. Focus on quick selections and not forcing the user to make multiple choices.

Page Loops

Apps that focus on recording the user performing an activity traditionally implement a page loop interface. When the user is in a page loop, the user interface presents a set of pages of information that provide different data and insights to the user. There are standard behaviors for going to the next and previous pages as well as starting and pausing/stopping the activity. These behaviors can change per product.

Menus

All products provide a standard behavior for the user to access the system menu. Your app should offer a menu of selections for configuration.

Widgets

Widgets live in a carousel of views that can be summoned at any time. Widgets function very similarly to device apps. The main difference is that the initial view has some key navigation taken away so the user can quickly navigate to other widgets.

The initial view of a widget should contain glanceable information, and should not be a menu of option. Menus cannot be navigated on button products because the up/down buttons have been repurposed.

Glances

Newer products have converted the widget display from a carousel of views to a list of active metrics. Each list entry, or glance, can provide a summary view to the user. If the user activates the glance, the app will launch.

Audio Content Providers

Audio content providers are launched from within the media player. The playback configuration context should allow for both selection of content for playback, and mechanisms for selecting content to download to the device.

Watch Faces

Watch faces are single view applications. They function as the home screen of the device when they are active. Because they are the longest running application on the watch, they have many restrictions in order to maximize battery life. Watch faces do not get user input from buttons or the touch screen, and they operate in a sleep mode for a majority of their running time.

System Settings

Garmin devices offer a number of global settings that are applied throughout the product experience, and your watch face should obey them as well. Time should obey the 12 and 24 hour mode. Distances and elevations should obey their respective metric or statute settings.

Low Power Mode

On all Garmin wearables, the user can activate the watch face with a turn of the wrist, causing it to exit sleep mode. During this period of time, the watch face comes alive. The UI refreshes once a second and the app can animate the canvas. When it reenters sleep mode, the watch face resumes once-a-minute updates in low-power mode.

Always-Active Mode (Connect IQ 2.3)

On wearables with memory in pixel (MIP) screens using Connect IQ 2.x or higher, watch faces can update once a second, but must put limits on the amount of screen they update. This partial update mechanism allows watch faces to implement always-on second hands.

AMOLED Watch Faces (Connect IQ 3.1)

On devices with AMOLED screens, always active mode works differently. When the device is in low power mode, the watch face can only update once a minute.

In addition, the watch face operates with screen protection enabled. Screen protection is designed to protect the life of the screen by enforcing a policy that any pixel is not left on for more than four minutes.

When screen protection is enabled, you should try to use as many black pixels as possible and not have any static elements like tick marks on the screen. You should also use thin fonts and potentially move your elements around on the screen every minute.

When the user gestures to look at their watch, the watch will exit sleep mode and your watch face can update the full display.

App Settings

If your watch face is configurable, you should add settings to the application so it can be configured using the Connect IQ Store application. Make sure your app responds immediately once the user changes a configuration.

Data Fields

Data fields are single-view plug-ins for the Garmin activity system. The user is allowed to place them in their activity profile (or multiple activity profiles). Here are some suggestions for creating them.

  • Support Multiple Placements Garmin activity profiles allow users to divide the screen in many ways. Do not assume the user will always use your data field as a full screen. Your app should have presentations for being very small, or having corners obscured by the round bezel.

  • Get the Users Attention Use the tone and vibe motor to alert the user when they have achieved a goal.

  • Use App Settings Allow the user to customize your view with app settings. Don’t require the user to install multiple versions of your app to get different presentations when possible.

Native Controls

With all of their variations in screen shapes and sensor offerings, designing an information architecture that works on all Garmin products can seem daunting. The Garmin UI toolkit provides a number of tools for making user interfaces that adapt to these variations.

Views

Views are canvases for your information. They can display text, graphics, animations and other content.

Menus

Menus offer a collection of selectable items for the user to choose from. A header is drawn at the top of the menu that offers the ability to give context to the selection.

The app can create a menu with a default presentation style that matches the device’s look and feel. If you want to match your branded look and feel, you can use a custom menu instead that allows you to custom render every menu element.

Menu Headers and Footers

By default menus loop from the last element on the list back to the first. A common pattern to break up long lists of information is to draw a custom footer element that hints at what lies beneath, and then switch to a new menu if the user goes past the end.

Pickers

Sometimes selection depends on building upon a number of small selections. Entering a phone number, for example, involves entry of multiple digits. Presented hierarchically this can be overwhelming for the user.

The Garmin UI Picker offers a generic way for the user to perform multiple selections to build their input. The user is presented a title, what has been entered so far, the current entry and the ability to select by using a one-dimensional next/previous interface.

Confirmation

Confirmations provides the ability to ask the user a question and get a yes/no answer. These are useful when trying to add small bits of friction around big decisions.

Progress Bar

At points where your app needs to do something asynchronous, you should present some kind of indication that the user needs to wait. Progress bars provide a simple full page experience that communicates progress. If you don’t have a known progress end point, you can use an infinite progress mode.

Maps (Connect IQ 3)

For products with onboard cartography, the Garmin UI allows you to push the native map view to the screen. You can overlay your own graphic elements over the map and change the view of the map to design your UI around it.

Points and Polylines

Maps allow bitmaps to be geo-located on the map to highlight points of interest. Maps also allow poly-lines to be overlaid for display of courses and boundaries.

Previewing, Browsing and Tracking

Map views can be used in three contexts. A map can be pushed to the screen that previews a given area. In this mode, user input is directed to any interface elements on screen. When browsing, the user has the ability to pan and zoom the map to control the focus areas. Finally, tracking will center the map on the user’s GPS location.

Designing for Buttons and Touch Screens

Garmin designs products with the use case in mind, and one key consideration is if the product is touchscreen or push-button. For those who have primarily developed in the mobile space physical buttons can be foreign. Touchscreens function well in indoor environments, but can be difficult to use with gloves, when wet, or when your body is in motion. For some environments the tactile feedback of buttons makes for a better experience.

This can be a challenge when designing an application experience. Connect IQ does allow for separate, tailored experiences for touch screens versus push-button devices, but this can double the return on investment. You can choose to support only touch screens or buttons, but that will severely limit your potential audience. The best approach is to use existing tools and patterns to support both push-button and touch-screen with a single interface when possible.

Selectables

The Garmin UI toolkit allows you to denote the clickable areas of a screen. On touch screens these will be tapp-able, but on push-button products the user can use the up/down buttons to switch between the elements, and the action button to click an element.

With this kind of interface, try to have a clear one-dimensional path through the elements for push-button products.

If the user has to select between over four choices, consider using a menu instead of a single page

Use Native Elements when Possible

Connect IQ provides different native elements for user input. These elements will always render using the native interface style and user-input options. Plus, they are guaranteed to be available on new products, which simplifies porting to the latest devices.

  • Use high contrast to make information pop when on screen.
  • Don’t depend on the display always remaining on.

Color

Proper use of color supports your branding and aids in communicating information.

Different Garmin devices have different visual themes. Some devices go for dark text on light backgrounds, while others use a light text on a dark background:

Use Color to Make Information Stand Out

In both visual themes, make sure you choose background and foreground color combinations that offer high contrast.

Consider Color Blindness and Cultural Meanings

Make sure the color choices you make work for all your users.

Animations

Good use of motion in your app can bring life to a static experience.

Page Transitions

The Garmin user interface toolkit offers a number of transitions to animate your views on and off screen. Pages can:

  • Immediately appear.
  • Slide from the left, right, top or bottom
  • Blink onto the screen.

You can use these transitions to push a page on top of a page, popping a page away to reveal the page below, or replacing the current page with another one.

Use Transitions to Suggest Page Hierarchy

A common pattern in Garmin apps is to have a primary view of information, and allow interaction via a menu hierarchy. When pushing the menus on screen have them slide in from the right. Then, when the user dismisses them, they can slide away to the right.

Use Transitions to Suggest Page Loops

Another common pattern in Garmin apps is the page loop, where the main screen offers a carousel of information that the user can loop through. With this pattern, either the up/down buttons or up/down swipes allow navigation between the pages. You can use sliding page transitions to give the illusion of this carousel of pages.

Animations

Connect IQ 3 introduces the ability for apps to incorporate animated sequences into their apps. These sequences can bring visual pop to your applications

Don’t hold the user hostage to your creativity. Keep animations short so they can get back to your app.

Typography

There are two kinds of system fonts on a Garmin device: The text font and the number font. The text font should be used for textual data and labels, while the number font is used for numerical data.

The system fonts that come with the device have been tested for readability for the device, and should be used whenever possible.

System Text Font

The system font typeface can vary by device, though typically Roboto is used. The system text font comes in the following sizes:

Extra Tiny Tiny Small Medium Large
Use for legal text Use for text labels Use for fitting long strings into an area Use for shorter strings Use for large textual information

System Number Font

The system number typeface can vary by device. This is the font that will be used by Garmin activities for displaying numerical information, and it’s useful for making values pop on screen. The number font supports numeric glyphs, colons, periods and dashes, but not text glyphs.

Custom Typefaces

Connect IQ allows developers to import a typeface into an app. These typefaces only allow for one point size to be imported.

Custom typefaces should be used to give a visual accent or to add a branding element to your app.
